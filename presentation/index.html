BASECAMP

The first camp of the Alpine Club of Canada,
Yoho Pass, British Columbia, July 8–16, 1906 —
reconstructed from primary sources.

This reconstruction is built entirely from museum, archive, and gallery holdings: Arthur Wheeler's unpublished handwritten daytimer at the Whyte Museum; the 1906 peer-reviewed Vaux brothers glacier paper; Byron Harmon's original negatives; Edward Feuz's guide fonds; and F.W. Freeborn's ACC albums. No source is quoted that cannot be pointed at directly in a public archive.

Yoho Pass · 51.470°N 116.555°W · 2,195 m 120 years on
Section I — The Killshot

Two primary sources. Same day. Same glacier. Same people.

How we know what happened on Sunday, July 15, 1906.

Wheeler's handwritten daytimer

Whyte Museum · M546 / item 13 · p. 197 · pencil on ruled diary paper
FIG. 1 — A.O. Wheeler, Personal Daytimer 1906, Sunday, July 15.
Up early & walked to Yoho Glacier — there worked all day at Glacier setting out plates & marking rocks — taking photographs at every point & plotted by [horse?] & instrumental readings.

Vaux Bros. there helping.

Walked back to Yoho Camp — had a lunch at the Vaux Camp as we passed. Arrived at Yoho Camp [where] service was being held.

Transcribed by visual reading; the daytimer is in pencil cursive and OCR cannot read it. See full transcription, July 7–17.

The Vaux brothers' 1906 glacier paper

Proc. Acad. Nat. Sci. Philadelphia · Dec. 1906 · pp. 568–579 · Internet Archive jstor-4063109
PAGE 578 · WAPTA OR YOHO GLACIER · YOHO VALLEY, NEAR FIELD, BRITISH COLUMBIA

"This glacier, located at the head of the Yoho Valley, is a very long day's trip from Field, the station on the railway. On July 15, 1906, almost a month earlier in the season than on the occasion of the previous visit, the glacier was again observed and the distance measured as 76 feet 7 inches, or about 124 feet less than in 1904. Allowing for the earlier date, it may be said that the tongue is in the same position as two years ago. The general aspect of the ice showed that it was shrinking and retreating, and this was particularly the case on the right side where the main stream debouches from a most beautiful ice arch."

— George Vaux, Jr. and William S. Vaux
FIG. 2 — "Observations Made in 1906 on Glaciers in Alberta and British Columbia," p. 578.
The Vaux brothers published this measurement in Philadelphia five months after Wheeler wrote his diary entry. Neither author could have seen the other's text. Each is an independent primary source for the same field day.

PDF of full 26-page paper — open access via Internet Archive.

The first ACC camp was not only a climbing holiday. For three full days of its second week it was a working glaciological field station, producing a peer-reviewed measurement — 76 feet 7 inches from a 1901 bedrock mark — that became the foundational datum of a multi-decade glacier-change record the Vaux family would continue until 1913 and the Mountain Legacy Project would revisit in the 21st century.

eve of camp

Wheeler rides out to the CPR hotel to meet supplies.

Went in to Field again and brought [Clover and Oliver] out to Camp. McDowell [P.D.?] came to Camp. Supplies arrived. Everything going on. Walter Fynn. More guides arrived.
On the ground: Swiss guide Walter Fynn and additional climbing staff assemble. Camp director Wheeler shuttles supplies from the CPR's Mount Stephen House in Field down to the Yoho Pass site.
Sunday
08
JULY 1906
★ camp opens

Opening day. The first ascent of Mt. Burgess is a trail-scouting mission.

Working at Camp all day.

[Wilfrid?] — Edward Feuz, Dr. Herdman & [Christian?] Feuz made the first climb of the peak Burgess to pick out the trails.

Showers during afternoon.

A large number of people arrived at CPR Hotel & [filed?] due for the Camp.
On the ground: The ACC's inaugural day is a logistical and reconnaissance operation, not a celebration. The lead guides — Edward Feuz, Christian Feuz, and Dr. J.C. Herdman — climb Mt. Burgess not for sport but to pick routes for the rest of the week. Afternoon thunder. Attendees disembark at Field and are funneled up to the pass.
Wheeler daytimer, July 8, 1906
Monday
09
JULY 1906

4 a.m. start. Wheeler gives his ponies to visitors.

Up at 4 a.m. — Rode in to Field — Met a lot of people riding and driving out to the Camp. When I got to the Hotel found that they had nearly all gone. Gave two of my ponies to visitors and [then] had looked after the luggage. Returned by [rig] to Chalet. From here walked to Camp — overtaking Miss Campbell on the way. Spent balance of the day at Camp making [it ready].
On the ground: Camp fully populated by nightfall. Wheeler himself arrived on foot after making the entire transfer happen.
Tuesday
10
JULY 1906

First official climbs dispatched. Mt. Stephen. Mt. President.

Got first party off on official climb — [Mt. Stephen]. Got party off on climb of Mt. Pres[ident]. Dr. Herdman took party to Emerald Glacier. Busy all day settling things and getting ready for Yoho trip tomorrow.

Big gathering around Camp fire at night.

Thunder & lightning during afternoon. Rain heavy.
On the ground: Three ropes on three peaks. Afternoon electrical storm. Evening campfire — the first ACC tradition.
Wednesday
11
JULY 1906

First Yoho Valley party departs. Mt. Wapta climbed.

Got official climbing party off [on tour]. As soon as possible got off 1st Yoho party. Dr. Herdman off with a party to the Emerald Glacier.

Day fine. People coming and going. Every one happy and pleased. Climb of Mt. Wapta.
Thursday
12
JULY 1906

Second Mt. Wapta climb. Herdman → Mt. Field. Heelis → Lookout Point.

Official climbing party off as usual. Yoho party started in good time. Climb of Mt. Wapta. Mr. Herdman took a party up Mt. Field. [Heelis?] took a Party to Lookout Pt.

Day showery but generally fine. Big gathering around camp fire last night.
Friday
13
JULY 1906
★ coleman auction

A.P. Coleman auctions ice axes, raising over $75 — the first ACC fundraiser.

Official and Yoho parties went off as usual. Other small parties here & there during the day. Mrs. Bridgland made [solo] climb of Mt. Wapta.

[Sale] of ice axes by Mr. Coleman — realized over $75.00.

Good time around the Camp fire afterwards.
On the ground: This is likely the earliest recorded ACC fundraising event. $75 in 1906 is roughly $2,500 in 2026 dollars. Mrs. Bridgland's solo Wapta climb — Morrison Parsons Bridgland's wife — is not listed in the camp roster but is recorded here in Wheeler's hand.
Saturday
14
JULY 1906

Last official climb. Wheeler bivouacs under a tree at Laughing Falls.

Last Official Climb took place today — Trip round Yoho. Packing Section went with material to mark [Glacier]. Camped for the night at the usual stopping place — the Laughing Falls. Clara and [Forrester] with me — slept under a tree.

Day very fine & hot.
On the ground: The "Packing Section went with material to mark Glacier" — this is the support party advancing equipment for the Vaux-Wheeler glacier survey to take place the next day. Clara is Wheeler's wife. He slept under a tree.
Sunday
15
JULY 1906
★ glacier day

The Vaux brothers and Wheeler survey Yoho Glacier all day.

Up early & walked to Yoho Glacier — there worked all day at Glacier setting out plates & marking rocks — taking photographs at every point & plotted by [horse] & instrumental readings.

Vaux Bros. there helping.

Walked back to Yoho Camp — had a lunch at the Vaux Camp as we passed. Arrived at Yoho Camp [where] service was being held.
On the ground: The measurement taken this day — 76 feet 7 inches from the 1901 bedrock mark — is published later that year in the Proceedings of the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia. It is the cross-source confirmation at the heart of this reconstruction. There were two camps: the main ACC Yoho Camp and a separate Vaux family camp. Divine service was held at the main camp on Sunday evening; the glaciology team arrived back late.
Wheeler daytimer, July 15, 1906 — the Vaux entry
Monday
16
JULY 1906
★ camp closes

Camp breaks up. Mt. Marpole party leaves late without guides.

Camp commenced to break up. Parties making climb of Mt. Marpole left very late — no guides. Commenced to take tents down. Busy all day helping people going away, settling their accounts.

Some rain in afternoon. Marpole party got back late — drenched to the skin but very joyful. Came home very jolly & supper — Yoho.
Tuesday
17
JULY 1906
the last to leave

Elizabeth Parker was the last attendee to leave the Yoho Pass camp.

Busy all day dismantling camp. All people gone except Miss Parker & the Parkers. Fine day. Got most of tents down. Turned [over] supplies to cook Rankin.
On the ground: Elizabeth Parker — the Manitoba Free Press journalist who had first proposed the founding of a Canadian alpine club in 1905 — was still there on the morning after everyone else had gone. She and her daughter Jean were the final departures from the ACC's first camp.
Section III — The People

IIITwenty attendees, in their own archives.

Thirty-three people were at the 1906 camp. This reconstruction has located digital archival holdings for twenty of them across two institutions: the Whyte Museum of the Canadian Rockies in Banff, and the Internet Archive's open-access scans of period scientific papers. The other thirteen attendees — guides and guests whose papers either never survived or remain undigitized — are named in the camp roster but have no current digital trace.

Arthur Oliver Wheeler

Camp director · ACC co-founder

Dominion Land Surveyor, driving force behind the ACC's founding in 1906. Directed the camp, managed logistics, and personally participated in the glacier survey on July 15.

3 items · 129 MB
Whyte M546 — Daytimer 1906 (359 pp.), Scrapbook Vol. 1, Wheeler Family Album

George Vaux, Jr.

Glaciologist · photographer · Philadelphia

Lawyer by profession, amateur glaciologist by passion. Co-author of the 1906 paper that corroborates Wheeler's diary entry to the exact day. Attended with his sister Mary and brother William.

35 items · 25 MB · 25 confirmed 1906/Yoho
Whyte V653 Vaux family photographs

William S. Vaux, Jr.

Engineer · photographer · glaciologist

The youngest Vaux sibling, engineer, photographer. Published multiple glacier observation papers between 1899 and 1907. Died in 1908, two years after the camp.

5 papers · 9 MB
Internet Archive: 1899, 1900, 1901, 1906, 1907 glacier papers

Mary M. Vaux

Botanical artist · glaciologist

Painted Rocky Mountain wildflowers that would eventually form her five-volume North American Wild Flowers at the Smithsonian. Later married Charles Doolittle Walcott of Burgess Shale fame — but in 1906 she was simply one of three Vaux siblings camped at Yoho.

10 items · 11 MB
Whyte V653 Vaux photographs

Elizabeth Parker

ACC co-founder · journalist · last to leave

Manitoba Free Press journalist whose 1905 editorial proposing a Canadian alpine club led directly to the founding of the ACC. Last person to leave the 1906 camp — still there with her daughter Jean on the morning of July 17.

3 items
Whyte V14 Parker display photographs, ACC fonds — annual meeting photograph identifying her as secretary

Edward Feuz, Jr.

Swiss guide · first Mt. Burgess ascent

Lead CPR Swiss guide. Made the first climb of Mt. Burgess on opening day with his brother Christian and Dr. Herdman — not for sport, but to pick out the trails the camp would use all week.

Byron Harmon

Photographer · ACC official photographer

Official photographer of the 1906 camp. His Whyte Museum negatives include numbered 1906 camp photographs and views of climbing on the Yoho Glacier.
