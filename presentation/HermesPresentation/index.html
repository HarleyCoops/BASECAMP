01 / FIELD STATION FEVER
“Some were armed with ice-axes and alpen-stocks—and umbrellas.”Frank Yeigh, First Camp

Before the sublime, the logistics are ridiculous.

The opening feeling is not solemn heritage. It is bodies and baggage, fever and comic equipment: a railway platform where Canada rehearses mountain nationhood with horses, wagons, cooks, tents, flags, and 40-pound limits.

Arrival motif: railway enables wilderness and threatens to consume it
02 / THE DELTA EDITS THE MAP
“After the delta, the deluge.”
Emerald Lake looks like a postcard.
Then the bridges are gone.
Then pack ponies become bridges.
Then the trail climbs 1,000 feet through wet spruce.
A route is not a line. It is a negotiation with water.
03 / A TEMPORARY REPUBLIC

A town of canvas appears at 6,000 feet.

Residence Park. Official Square. Horse paddock. Dining tent for one hundred. Bulletin board. Camp fire. Speeches. Jokes. Bunting. A club turns wilderness into civic theatre for one week, then disappears.

Camp as pop-up city: social heat at night, scientific cold by day
04 / NO SKIRTS ON THE ROPE

The most radical artifact is a clothing rule.

“No lady climbing, who wears skirts, will be allowed to take a place on a rope…” The line reads comic now. In 1906 it is safety policy, gender technology, and permission structure all at once.

05 / THE VICE-PRESIDENT AS EXAM

The mountain becomes a credentialing machine.

Five official ascents. A threshold above 9,000 feet. Rock, snow, ice, ridge, rest places. The archive says 42 in one place and 44 in another. Keep the contradiction: membership is a story told by tired people.

06 / AGAINST MAMMON

Elizabeth Parker writes an anti-tourism manifesto inside a tourism machine.

“To popularize mountaineering is not to vulgarize… the way of the monster, Mammon.”

The railway makes the camp possible. The club immediately fears what steam, electricity, hotels, and commerce will do to the solitude it loves. This contradiction should stay loud.

Preservation vs access is already present at the founding

08 / THE GLACIER CLOCK

On July 15, two primary sources click into place.

Wheeler’s daytimer: “Vaux Bros. there helping.” Vaux paper: “76 feet 7 inches.” The camp was not merely recreation; it was a working scientific station.

11 / THE ARCHIVE DISAGREES

Closing day: the neat story falls apart.

Wheeler’s diary hints the Marpole party left late “no guides” and came back drenched but joyful. The official report names the Feuz guides. The archive is weather. It fogs, clears, contradicts itself, and still leaves a route.

12 / THE LAST NEGATIVE

The archive ends in white space.

The final contact sheet is almost empty: one surviving canoe image floating in the blank. After all the abundance, this emptiness feels honest. A corpus is never complete. A mountain keeps most of what happened.

Abundance and absence belong together
