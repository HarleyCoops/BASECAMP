# BASECAMP — Cross-Source Primary Findings

## Finding #1 — July 15, 1906 Yoho Glacier field day (★★★★★)

**Two independent primary sources corroborate each other to the exact day.**

### Source A — Handwritten field diary
- **Author:** Arthur Oliver Wheeler (camp director)
- **Document:** Personal Daytimer 1906, Whyte Museum M546 / item 13
- **Date:** Sunday, July 15, 1906
- **Location:** `people/arthur-wheeler/artifacts/descriptions23111_M546_13.pdf` p.197
- **Transcription:**
> "Up early & walked to Yoho Glacier — there worked all day at Glacier setting out plates & marking rocks — taking photographs at every point & plotted by [horse?] & instrumental readings. **Vaux Bros. there helping**. Walked back to Yoho Camp. Had a lunch at the Vaux Camp as we passed. Arrived at Yoho Camp [where] service was being held."

### Source B — Published peer-reviewed measurement
- **Authors:** George Vaux Jr. and William S. Vaux
- **Document:** "Observations Made in 1906 on Glaciers in Alberta and British Columbia"
- **Journal:** Proceedings of the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia, December 1906, pp. 568-579
- **Location:** `people/george-vaux/artifacts/ia_1906_observations_George_Vaux_Jr_1906_GLACIERS.pdf` p.578
- **Quote (section "WAPTA OR YOHO GLACIER — YOHO VALLEY, NEAR FIELD, BRITISH COLUMBIA"):**
> "On **July 15, 1906**, almost a month earlier in the season than on the occasion of the previous visit, the glacier was again observed and the distance measured as **76 feet 7 inches**, or about 124 feet less than in 1904. Allowing for the earlier date, it may be said that the tongue is in the same position as two years ago. The general aspect of the ice showed that it was shrinking and retreating, and this was particularly the case on the right side where the main stream debouches from a most beautiful ice arch."

### Corroborated facts
1. Wheeler and the Vaux brothers were together at the Wapta/Yoho Glacier on July 15, 1906.
2. The fieldwork was a re-measurement of a reference mark set earlier (Wheeler: "marking rocks... instrumental readings"; Vaux: "distance measured as 76 feet 7 inches" from the bedrock mark placed August 17, 1901).
3. There were TWO distinct camps — a main ACC "Yoho Camp" and a separate "Vaux Camp" for the Vaux family glaciology operations.
4. Divine service was held at the main Yoho Camp that Sunday afternoon; the glaciology team returned late.
5. The Yoho Glacier tongue was at the same position as 1904 allowing for season, but shrinking in mass — a "beautiful ice arch" at the right-side outlet.

### Why this matters
This is the earliest documented instance of coordinated primary glacier monitoring at Yoho, linking the ACC's inaugural camp directly to the Vaux family's multi-decade glacier-change photographic record — which itself became the foundation of the Mountain Legacy Project's 20th/21st-century repeat-photography research. The 1906 camp was not merely a recreational climbing event; it was a working scientific field station for the first three full days of its second week.

## Secondary findings from the Wheeler daytimer

(See `people/arthur-wheeler/daytimer_pages/TRANSCRIPTION_1906_camp_window.md` for full text.)

- **Jul 8** — First ascent of Mt. Burgess for trail reconnaissance by Edward Feuz + Christian Feuz + Dr. Herdman.
- **Jul 10** — Mt. Stephen and Mt. President official climbs dispatched.
- **Jul 11-13** — Three separate Mt. Wapta ascents in three days.
- **Jul 13** — A.P. Coleman auctioned ice axes in camp, raising over $75 (possible first ACC fundraising event).
- **Jul 14** — Mrs. Bridgland climbed Mt. Wapta (possibly the camp's first recorded women's ascent of Wapta).
- **Jul 16** — Camp closed; final Mt. Marpole climb departed late without guides and returned drenched but happy.
- **Jul 17** — Elizabeth Parker was the last to leave.
