# Elizabeth Parker (1856–1944)

## Role at 1906 Yoho Camp
**First Secretary of the Alpine Club of Canada.** Co-founder and key organizer of the inaugural 1906 Yoho Pass camp (July 8–18, 1906). Physically attended the camp with her daughter Jean Parker. Represented a watershed moment for women's full participation in mountaineering: the ACC admitted 15 women out of 44 founding members, establishing it as gender-inclusive from inception.

## Bio Hook
Journalist, mountaineer, and co-founder of Canada's first national alpine club. Elizabeth Fulton Parker (1856–1944) came to Winnipeg in 1892 and began her 36-year journalism career at the Manitoba Free Press in 1904, writing under the pen name "The Bookman." In a March 1906 editorial, she fiercely opposed a proposal to create a Canadian chapter of the American Alpine Club, invoking national pride: "It knocks me speechless and fills me shame for young Canada … Surely, between Halifax and Victoria, there can be found at least a dozen persons who are made of the stuff needed to climb, and care enough about our mountain heritage." Her advocacy sparked the founding of the **Alpine Club of Canada** on March 27–28, 1906, alongside surveyor Arthur Oliver Wheeler. She served as the club's first Secretary through 1908 and remained a towering figure in Canadian mountaineering until her death. Designated a National Historic Person by Parks Canada (2011). The **Elizabeth Parker Hut** in Yoho National Park (built 1919, donated by Canadian Pacific Railway) honors her legacy.
